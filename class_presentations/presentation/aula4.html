Lecture 4: Differential Kinematics


Differential Kinematics


We know how to describe the pose of the end-effector relative to a reference frame \(\mathcal{F}_0\).

The direct kinematics maps the joint “position” (configuration) to the rigid body “position” (pose).

Now we want to map not the “positions” but the “velocities.” That is the goal of differential kinematics.


The S Operator


The S Operator

Definition

Before proceeding, we must introduce an important concept:

Definition: Let \(a = (a_x \ a_y \ a_z)^T\) be a 3D vector. We define the function \(S: \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 1} \mapsto \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}\) as: $$S(a) = \Large{\Bigg(}\begin{array}{ccc} 0 & -a_z & a_y \\ a_z & 0 & -a_x \\ -a_y & a_x & 0 \end{array}\Large{\Bigg)}.$$

The name “S” comes from skew-symmetric.

Conclusion

Having the geometric Jacobian, we can map joint velocities into velocities (linear and angular) of the end-effector.

This is very useful because it is often easier to plan control in velocity space, while the robot receives joint velocity commands. This mapping solves the problem.

In the next lecture, we will see that the geometric Jacobian will be used as a main ingredient to compute other Jacobians relative to a space where velocity planning is even simpler than in velocity space.

In that space, we will plan our controller.
